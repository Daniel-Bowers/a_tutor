Beginner's Portrait Photography Shopping List

This list was made with thrifty photographers, focusing on portraits, in mind. I tried to order them by decreasing necessity and versatility, and increasing cost.
My apologies: The dropdown menus do not currently work. They contain explanations.

  • all along: develop your skill (free) ▼
    • read good photo websites like Cambridge in Colour and Strobist
    • any photo you see: analyze it
    • learn about body language
  • Gimp or other open source photo editing software (free or donation) ▼
    • disclaimer: I have basically no experience with Gimp
    • learn basic techniques and their limits, like blemish removal, color correction
    • if you stay serious about photography, you'll probably want to get Photoshop, since GIMP doesn't handle RAW files (which almost all pros use rather than JPGs)
  • white piece of paper ▼
    • use to correct the color balance of photos
    • first shoot with paper in the picture, then follow up with the "real" photos
    • other white objects can also work
    • professional white cards are likely better, but this is good enough for now
  • Nikon D70, D70s or other older DSLR with kit lens (<$150) ▼
    • Nikon D70 has excellent flash synch speed of 1/500 sec; not important to know now but you may find this feature useful later on
    • kit lenses tend to be very high value for the cost
    • I own a D70. I later bought a more advanced camera later, but have kept the D70 for a backup, sacrificial, quickly accessible, and/or high-synch-speed camera. Generally it's good to stick with one brand but since you're not spending too much on this camera, it shouldn't be a big deal.
  • foil tape ($1, 99¢ Only) ▼
    • tape to camera to bounce flash or trigger an optical flash
    • "bounce flash" is when you reflect the light off ceilings or walls to light the subject; it softens the shadows, and usually adds fill and brings key and fill light levels closer together
    • you can also use your hand, but it's troublesome
  • white foam core, 20"x30" ($1, dollar store) ▼
    • diffuse reflector for fill light
    • flag to block light
    • background for adult head shots or child head and chest shots
    • create a breeze for the subject's hair
    • better for filling/shading head and upper torso; could be used to fill-light an adult body but would likely be weak or uneven (never tried it)
  • glasses with lenses removed ($1 each, dollar store)▼
    • glare on glasses usually looks horrible
    • viewers will assume glasses they see in a photo have lenses
    • kids like wearing glasses
    • buy the most generic silver reading glasses with thin frames first
    • buy thicker black-framed glasses next
    • drill holes through lenses if they don't easily come out with effort
  • windshield reflector ($1, dollar store) ▼
    • harder reflector than white foam core
    • probably not as good for creating breeze
    • may be a full or partial flag—how transparent is it?
    • better for filling/shading small or medium areas depending on size
  • black foam core, 20"x30" ($1, dollar store) ▼
    • flag to block light
    • background for adult head shots or child head and chest shots
    • create a breeze for the subject's hair
    • better for filling/shading head and upper torso
  • Starburst ($1 box, dollar store)▼
    • bribe kids
  • extra subject to help (free, favor, small fee) ▼
    • photograph two people separately; one holds background/reflector/flag while other is photographed
    • kids will help for candy or small rewards
  • set of small to medium clamps ($5, Harbor Freight Tools) ▼
    • clamp backgrounds, flags, fill objects
    • takes the place of a person
  • white bedsheet or pillow case ▼
    • diffuses light
    • David Hobby says white cloth can mimic expensive softboxes
    • clamp to poles or other objects
  • bungee ball cords (<$5 Walmart) ▼
    • I use these frequently
    • you can secure the sheet to poles, flashes to stands, etc.
  • transluscent shower curtain ($5, Walmart) ▼
    • big diffuser for softening shadows
    • lowers the light level somewhat
  • various colors of poster board, ~20"x30" ($.50 each, dollar store or sale at Hobby Lobby) ▼
    • clamp to foam core as background
    • would probably be possible to clamp two side by side to a larger foam core (~3'ft'×4') and edit out the edge down the middle of the photo
  • flashlight ($1, dollar store)▼
    • light painting
    • help see equipment and help camera focus in oft-dark conditions
  • flash stand ($15 Amazon or specific online retail store) ▼
    • buy two if you can find a package deal
    • can clamp all sorts of things to these besides flashes
  • used framed window screen (free) ▼
    • scrim(?): cuts light while changing softness little
    • can darken a background if out of focus, but my experiments with this have not been successful
    • convenient for clamping to cloths to act as diffuser, etc.
    • clip posterboard(s), cloth to it as background
  • black foam paper(?) ($4 Michael's) ▼
    • squishy black foam pad
    • DIY lens hoods, flash snoots, etc.
    • this might be it
  • Neewer TT560 Flash Speedlite ($30 Amazon) ▼
    • cheap, good, simple
    • built in optical slave, which is necessary if it is to be used off-camera without a radio trigger
    • doesn't seem to be powerful compared to other flashes I own
    • can be triggered like a radio trigger (somewhat) by shining a laser into the sensor, but this is probably inconvenient
  • 4xAA batteries, rechargeable ($6 Harbor Freight Tools) ▼
    • may not be what David Hobby recommends here but they've worked fine for me so far
  • tripod ($10 Craigslist) ▼
    • "The Most Powerful Light in Your Bag"
    • self-portraits
    • light painting
  • remote shutter release (pretty cheap) ▼
    • will look something like this
    • used for self-portraits and situations where you need a very still camera
    • corded triggers are also available, but are less versatile
  • twine ($1 dollar store?)▼
    • versatile
    • self-portraits
  • recharger ($7 Harbor Frieght Tools) ▼
    • faster chargers like these wear down batteries faster; I've never had batteries die yet, though, so it probably takes a long time
    • David Hobby likes this one and this one
  • gel set ($10 online?) ▼
    • some suggestions
    • CTOs are most useful; CTBs are next useful
  • 4×6 photo album ($1, dollar store) ▼
    • use to store your gels
  • sticky-back velcro (≈$1 Walmart) ▼
    • attach to gels like this
    • I recommend the scratchy style go on the gel, the soft side go on the flash
  • DIY spot grid (≈free)▼
    • glue together pieces of cardboard and attach to flash with a rubber band
    • narrows the beam; the deeper the pieces, the more narrow the beam
    • may not be as good as purchased grid
  • flash/umbrella bracket ($8 Amazon) ▼
    • I prefer ones with long levers and cold shoes that can be tightened to the flash, not just vice versa
  • shoot-through umbrella (≥$10 Amazon) ▼
    • the bigger the better, in my opinion
    • shoot through it with flash
    • shade subjects outside
  • polarizer filter for kit lens ($15 Amazon) ▼
    • explanation
    • cuts light in half in general; cuts strongly the light in a particular direction
    • a must-have for outdoors, or shooting people wearing real glasses in some situations
    • darken skies, making clouds pop and compressing the dynamic range
    • saturate outdoor colors
    • reduce glare on water
    • see through water, windows, and people's glasses
    • reduce glare on skin oil
  • voltmeter▼
    • use to test your batteries before shoots to be confident they'll work
    • I don't like the ones from Harbor Freight Tools
    • approximate voltages for NiMH batteries:
      • full: ≥ 1.36 V
      • good: 1.25-1.35 V
      • low: < 1.25 V
  • radio trigger and receiver ($15 Amazon) ▼
    • get one with good average rating
    • more versatile than optical triggers
  • PVC pipe and elbows ($20? hardware store) ▼
    • bedsheet backgrounds
      • make rectangle
      • clamp on sheet evenly and tautly
      • spray sheet with water from spray jar to reduce wrinkles (ironing seems to be a waste of time)
      • dimensions limited to about five feet per side, I'd say; more than this and the PVC pipe sags
    • attach diffusers
      • three-way perpendicular elbows can help make light tents, but these can be made without PVC
  • monopod ($15, Walmart)▼
    • camera stabilization
    • extension pole for flash, like this
    • lighter, smaller, smaller footprint than tripod
  • cold shoe ($3 somewhere online)▼
    • goes between flash and 1/4 × 20 thread bolt used for monopods, etc.
    • useful for DIY items
    • I would buy at least two plus one for every DIY project you plan to do
  • 50mm f/1.8 (better) or f/2 (good) autofocus lens ($50-$100) ▼
    • be careful not to distort the face by getting too close
    • blurry backgrounds
    • low-light situations
